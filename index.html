The Death of Socrates
Socrates neither denied the existence of the gods nor disparaged them. He did, however, call on people to know themselves and cultivate themselves through science, a practice his opponents considered an insult to the gods and a threat to their interests. Therefore, they fabricated charges against him, and he was sentenced to death in 399 BC. Despite his friend Crito's attempts to persuade him to flee, Socrates refused, asserting that fleeing would contradict his teachings and that he did not want to break the laws, even if they were against him. He calmly accepted death and drank the poison, continuing to teach until his final moments. -Analysis of the painting "The Death of Socrates" by Jacques-Louis David: The painting depicts the pivotal moment in Socrates' life, when he chose death by poison over exile.
Las Hilanderas or The Fable of Arachne
"Las Helanderas" or "The Tale of Arachne" is a painting by the Spanish artist Velázquez, painted between 1657 and 1658, and is on display at the Prado Museum in Madrid. The painting depicts the story of Arachne from Greek mythology, who challenged the goddess Athena to weave a web, only to be transformed into a spider by Athena. The painting expresses the intersection of "art and craft" and symbolizes that "creativity requires skill and technique." It is considered one of Velázquez's greatest and most complex works, along with "Las Meninas."
Last Supper
"The Last Supper" is a famous fresco painted by Leonardo da Vinci between 1495 and 1498 on a wall in the Monastery of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan, Italy. The painting depicts the Last Supper of Jesus Christ with his twelve disciples, as described in the Gospels, at the moment when he announced that "one of the disciples would betray him." The disciples' faces display expressions of astonishment, anger, and sadness, and each reacts in his own way, giving the painting a strong human and emotional character. - It is considered one of the greatest works of the Renaissance. - It is distinguished by its realism of emotions, the organization of artistic composition, and the use of perspective to give depth to the scene.
